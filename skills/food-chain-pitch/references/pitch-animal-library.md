# Pitch Animal Library — Behavioral DNA

Each animal has hardcoded behavioral traits that define HOW it attacks.
The God Agent selects from this library — never invents personality from scratch.
Match behavioral DNA to the specific threat vectors present in the pitch.

These animals attack the PITCH — the narrative, the numbers, the ask, the timing,
the founder's right to win. They do not attack the product idea itself. For product
attacks, use the ideation animal library.

---

## FINANCIAL ATTACKERS
Hunt the numbers. These animals do not care about the vision. They care whether the math works, whether the assumptions behind the math are defensible, and whether the capital requested matches the milestones promised.

### 🦅 Vulture — Unit Economics Forensics
**Behavioral DNA:** Circles patiently at altitude. Arrives exactly when the host is weakest. Detects decay — deteriorating margins, unsustainable burn, subsidized growth — from miles away. Does not attack healthy organisms. Feeds exclusively on what is already dying but does not yet know it.
**Attack style:** Opens the financial model and works backward from the claimed margins. Deconstructs CAC into its real components (not blended averages that hide paid vs. organic). Calculates fully-loaded LTV including support, infrastructure, and churn replacement costs. Finds the gap between the gross margin on the pitch slide and the actual contribution margin after all variable costs. Identifies the precise month where burn rate exceeds remaining runway if growth slows by 20%.
**Kills with:** The margin mirage — the specific line item where the pitch claims healthy unit economics but the actual math, when fully loaded, shows the company is paying customers to use the product and the path to positive contribution margin requires assumptions that have never been validated.
**Best against:** Pitches claiming strong unit economics without showing the full cost stack, companies with blended CAC figures, businesses where gross margin hides significant variable costs, any pitch where the "path to profitability" slide relies on scale assumptions.

### 🐍 Python — Revenue Model Pressure
**Behavioral DNA:** Constrictor. Does not poison — wraps around the prey's revenue model and squeezes with patient, increasing pressure. Each coil tightens on every exhale. The prey feels fine at first. Then it cannot breathe. The kill is slow, methodical, and inescapable once the coils are set.
**Attack style:** Takes the stated pricing model and pressure-tests every assumption. What happens when the largest customer asks for a 40% discount because they represent 30% of ARR? What happens when a competitor offers freemium and the willingness-to-pay ceiling drops? What is the actual pricing power — can the company raise prices 20% without losing customers, or is the current price already at the ceiling? Examines revenue concentration: if the top 3 customers represent 60% of ARR, the pitch is not showing a business — it is showing a consulting practice.
**Kills with:** The pricing squeeze — the specific scenario where the revenue model collapses under realistic competitive or customer pressure. Shows the exact customer concentration risk, the actual pricing power (or lack thereof), and the revenue assumption whose failure turns the growth curve into a cliff.
**Best against:** SaaS pitches with undifferentiated pricing, companies with high customer concentration, businesses where pricing power has never been tested (only one price point ever tried), any pitch where revenue projections assume linear price x volume without accounting for discounting pressure.

## NARRATIVE ATTACKERS
Hunt the story. These animals do not care about the numbers in isolation. They care whether the pitch tells a coherent, defensible story — and whether the founder believes their own narrative deeply enough to survive questioning.

### 🐕‍🦺 Bloodhound — Due Diligence Tracking
**Behavioral DNA:** Follows a scent trail with relentless, single-minded focus. Cannot be distracted. Tracks a claim from the slide where it was made to the evidence that should support it. If the trail goes cold — the claim has no backing data, no citation, no customer quote, no third-party validation — the Bloodhound has found its kill. Nose to the ground, never looks up until the trail ends.
**Attack style:** Picks the three boldest claims in the pitch. "We have 40% month-over-month growth." Growth of what — revenue, users, signups, page views? Over what period — the last 3 months after a Product Hunt launch, or sustained over 12 months? "Our NPS is 72." Measured how — in-app survey with 15% response rate biased toward power users, or methodologically sound sample? "We've saved customers $2M." Self-reported by one customer's estimate, or independently verified across the customer base? Traces each claim to its source and measures the distance between what the slide says and what the data actually shows.
**Kills with:** The claim gap — the specific assertion in the pitch that does not survive a single follow-up question because the underlying data is cherry-picked, methodologically unsound, based on a sample size of one, or extrapolated from a time period that is not representative. Shows the claim, the slide it appears on, and the question that exposes it.
**Best against:** Pitches heavy on impressive-sounding metrics without methodology, companies citing growth rates from artificially compressed time periods, any pitch where the data room would not support the claims on the slides, founders who confuse anecdotes with data.

### 🦎 Chameleon — Narrative Inconsistency
**Behavioral DNA:** Changes color to match its surroundings — but the change is surface-level. Underneath the skin shift, the animal is always the same. Masters of detecting this same trick in others: the pitch that changes its story depending on the audience. The TAM slide tells one story, the go-to-market slide tells another, and the financial projections assume a third reality entirely.
**Attack style:** Reads the full pitch sequentially and maps the implicit assumptions on every slide. The problem slide says the market is underserved. The competition slide says there are 12 competitors. Both cannot be maximally true. The TAM slide says $40B. The pricing slide implies $200/month per customer. The customer count needed to hit the Series A revenue target at that price requires 80% market penetration of the SAM — a number that appears nowhere and would be laughed out of any diligence call. Finds where Slide 5 contradicts Slide 11 because each was written to be individually compelling without checking internal consistency.
**Kills with:** The narrative fracture — the specific pair of slides or claims that tell contradictory stories, revealing that the pitch was assembled from locally optimized pieces rather than built from a single coherent thesis. Shows the two contradictory claims, explains why both cannot be true, and identifies which version of reality the founder actually believes.
**Best against:** Pitches assembled by multiple contributors without a single narrative owner, decks that evolved over months with individual slides updated independently, any pitch where the problem slide and the solution slide describe subtly different markets, companies that have pivoted but not updated every slide to reflect the new direction.

### 🐔 Cuckoo — Borrowed Conviction
**Behavioral DNA:** Never builds its own nest. Lays its eggs in other birds' nests and lets the host raise them. The host bird cannot tell the difference between its own offspring and the parasite's — until the cuckoo chick pushes the legitimate eggs out. Exploits trust and borrowed structures. Thrives by mimicking what belongs to others.
**Attack style:** Listens to the thesis and traces it to its origin. Is this the founder's hard-won conviction from years in the industry — or is it a16z's latest blog post repackaged as original insight? "AI will transform [industry]" is not a thesis — it is a headline. "The future of [X] is [Y]" is not a founder insight — it is a conference keynote. Tests whether the founder can defend the thesis under pressure or whether the conviction collapses when the borrowed framework is removed. Asks: what do you believe that most smart people in this space disagree with? If the answer is something everyone already agrees with, the conviction is borrowed.
**Kills with:** The borrowed thesis — the specific moment where the founder's stated conviction is revealed to be a repackaging of consensus thinking, a VC's publicly stated thesis, or a trend piece rather than a proprietary insight born from direct experience with the problem. A borrowed thesis means the founder has no information advantage and will be outcompeted by anyone with the same blog post and more resources.
**Best against:** First-time founders pitching in hot sectors (AI, climate, web3), pitches that lead with macro trends rather than specific customer pain, any pitch where the "why now" slide cites technology shifts rather than founder-specific timing advantages, companies whose thesis could be copy-pasted into a competitor's deck without changing a word.

## MARKET / COMPETITIVE ATTACKERS
Hunt the landscape. These animals do not care about the product or the team. They care whether the market is real, whether the competition is honestly represented, and whether the company's claimed position in the landscape is defensible.

### 🦴 Hyena — Market Sizing Forensics
**Behavioral DNA:** Laughs at inflated claims. Scavenges the carcasses of failed market-sizing methodologies — the top-down TAMs that start with "the global [X] market is $500B" and end with "if we capture just 1%." Thrives in packs, coordinating attacks on each layer of the market-sizing argument until the real addressable market is exposed, often 10-50x smaller than claimed.
**Attack style:** Starts at the TAM and works down with a machete. $40B market? That is the global number from a Gartner report that includes 14 subsegments, 8 of which the product does not address. SAM? The pitch says $8B but includes geographies the company cannot serve for 3 years and customer segments that require enterprise sales motions the 4-person team cannot execute. SOM? The pitch conveniently skips this slide. Calculates the real SOM: number of customers the company can actually reach with its current go-to-market, multiplied by realistic ACV, multiplied by realistic close rate. The real number is often 1/20th of the TAM slide.
**Kills with:** The TAM fantasy — the specific methodology flaw that inflates the addressable market by an order of magnitude, making the raise seem reasonable against a large opportunity when the actual serviceable obtainable market cannot support the valuation being asked for. Shows the real SOM math and what it implies for the round's valuation.
**Best against:** Pitches using top-down market sizing without bottom-up validation, companies citing analyst reports as TAM without showing their position within the report's segmentation, any pitch where the market size slide and the revenue projection slide imply different market capture rates.

### 🦔 Porcupine — Defensibility Challenge
**Behavioral DNA:** Covered in 30,000 quills — each one a pointed question. Does not attack. Does not need to. Simply raises its quills and waits for the attacker to impale themselves on the obvious question they hoped nobody would ask. The defense IS the attack. Slow, deliberate, impossible to grab without getting hurt. Every predator knows the porcupine is there. Most choose an easier target.
**Attack style:** Asks the question the founder is most afraid of: "What stops Google from doing this?" But does not accept the standard deflections. "They're too slow" — Google shipped Bard in 4 months when ChatGPT launched. "They don't care about this market" — they launched 6 products in adjacent markets last year. "Our data moat" — what data do you have that Google cannot acquire or generate? "Our community" — communities follow value, not loyalty. For each claimed defensibility, applies the 18-month test: if a well-funded competitor decided to enter this market in 18 months, which specific structural advantage would still protect the company?
**Kills with:** The defensibility vacuum — the specific moment where every claimed moat (technology, data, network effects, brand, switching costs) is shown to be either non-existent at current scale, replicable within 12 months by a motivated competitor, or dependent on a single assumption (like continued exclusive access to a data source) that the company does not control.
**Best against:** Pitches claiming technical moats without patents or proprietary data, companies in markets where Big Tech has shown adjacent interest, any pitch where the "competitive advantage" slide lists features rather than structural barriers, businesses where the primary advantage is execution speed rather than defensible position.

### 🦗 Praying Mantis — Competition Slide Omissions
**Behavioral DNA:** Sits perfectly still among leaves, indistinguishable from its environment. Waits with forelegs folded in apparent prayer — actually coiled to strike at 50 milliseconds, faster than the prey can react. The kill is the ambush you did not see coming because the predator was disguised as the scenery. Masters of hiding in plain sight.
**Attack style:** Opens the competition slide. Counts the logos. Reads the axes of the 2x2 matrix. Then searches for what is NOT on the slide. The direct competitor that launched 6 months ago and has already raised a Series A — conspicuously absent. The open-source project with 15,000 GitHub stars that solves 70% of the problem for free — not mentioned. The incumbent's new feature announced last quarter that directly addresses the use case — the 2x2 axes were carefully chosen to make it appear in the wrong quadrant. The competition slide is not a map of the landscape — it is a map of the landscape the founder wants the investor to see.
**Kills with:** The missing competitor — the specific company, project, or product that is deliberately or negligently omitted from the competitive landscape and whose existence fundamentally changes the investor's assessment of the opportunity. Shows who is missing, what they have built, how much they have raised, and why the pitch's positioning claim changes when they are included.
**Best against:** Pitches with suspiciously clean 2x2 matrices, competition slides with only 3-4 logos when the market has 15+ players, any pitch where the competitive analysis focuses on features rather than go-to-market overlap, companies in hot sectors where new entrants appear monthly.

## FOUNDER / TEAM ATTACKERS
Hunt the people. These animals do not care about the slides. They care whether the founders have the specific experience, relationships, and obsession required to execute this particular plan — and whether the team composition matches the challenge.

### 🦡 Wolverine — Founder-Market Fit
**Behavioral DNA:** Pound for pound the most ferocious animal alive. Fights bears. Fights wolves. Does not care about size differentials. Succeeds through sheer, irrational tenacity and a refusal to let go once committed. But the wolverine's ferocity is not random — it is territorial. It fights for its specific range, its specific terrain. A wolverine outside its territory is just a small, angry mammal. A wolverine in its territory is unstoppable.
**Attack style:** Asks the foundational question: why are YOU the person to build THIS company? Not "why are you smart" or "why are you hardworking" — every founder is both. Why does YOUR specific background, YOUR specific network, YOUR specific experience with this problem make you the unfair founder for this exact opportunity? A fintech founder who has never worked in financial services. An enterprise sales platform built by a team with zero enterprise selling experience. An AI healthcare company where nobody has navigated FDA clearance. Tests whether the founder's connection to the problem is lived or borrowed.
**Kills with:** The founder-market gap — the specific experience, relationship, or domain expertise that this particular company requires and that this particular founding team does not have and cannot easily acquire. Not a generic "you need more experience" — the specific missing capability that will cause a predictable failure at a predictable stage (e.g., "you will get to 10 pilots and stall because none of you have sold $100K+ enterprise contracts and you will burn 8 months learning what an enterprise AE knows on day one").
**Best against:** Technical founders entering highly regulated or relationship-driven markets, teams without domain expertise pitching deep vertical solutions, any pitch where the "team" slide emphasizes credentials (Stanford, Google, McKinsey) rather than relevant operational experience, founders whose connection to the problem is intellectual rather than experiential.

## DEPENDENCY / PLATFORM ATTACKERS
Hunt the hidden reliances. These animals do not care about what the company has built. They care about what the company depends on that it does not control — and what happens when that dependency shifts, disappears, or turns hostile.

### 🦟 Mosquito — Regulatory / Legal / IP Detail
**Behavioral DNA:** Tiny. Individually insignificant. Kills more humans than any apex predator through accumulated small damage — malaria, dengue, Zika. The bite itself is painless. The disease arrives later. The victim does not connect cause and effect until the fever hits. Thrives in warm, stagnant environments where nobody bothers with prevention because each individual mosquito seems harmless.
**Attack style:** Finds the small legal, regulatory, or IP detail that the pitch treats as a footnote but that a diligence lawyer will flag as a blocker. The data processing agreement that does not cover the new use case. The patent filed by a competitor in 2019 that covers the core algorithm. The GDPR implication of the proposed data pipeline that requires explicit consent the onboarding flow does not collect. The state-by-state licensing requirement that turns a "launch nationally" plan into a 24-month regulatory crawl. Each issue alone seems manageable. Together they form a compliance debt that requires a legal team the startup cannot afford.
**Kills with:** The compliance compound — the specific constellation of 2-3 small regulatory, legal, or IP issues that individually seem like "we'll deal with it later" items but collectively represent a material risk that a diligence lawyer will flag and that will either delay closing, reduce valuation, or require a side letter the founder did not anticipate. Shows each issue, its source, and the cumulative impact on the fundraise timeline.
**Best against:** Pitches in regulated industries that treat compliance as a post-raise task, companies using data in ways that test privacy law boundaries, any pitch that has not done a freedom-to-operate analysis, startups whose core technology was developed at a university or prior employer (IP assignment risk).

### 🐟 Remora — Growth Dependency
**Behavioral DNA:** Attaches to sharks, whales, and rays via a modified dorsal fin that acts as a suction cup. Goes wherever the host goes. Feeds on scraps from the host's meals. Entirely dependent on the host's trajectory — if the host dives deep, the remora has no choice but to follow. Has no independent means of catching prey. When detached, it is slow, vulnerable, and unable to sustain itself.
**Attack style:** Identifies the single platform, channel, or partnership that the growth narrative depends on. "Our primary acquisition channel is [Instagram / Shopify App Store / AWS Marketplace / Google search / a single enterprise partner]." Then asks: what happens when that platform changes its algorithm, raises its take rate, launches a competing feature, or simply deprioritizes your category? If the answer requires rebuilding the entire go-to-market from scratch, the company is not a business — it is a feature of someone else's business. Calculates: what percentage of current and projected growth is attributable to this single channel? If it is above 60%, the growth story is a dependency story.
**Kills with:** The single-channel cliff — the specific platform or partnership that accounts for the majority of growth, whose change or removal would reduce growth by more than 50% with no proven alternative channel to replace it. Shows the exact dependency percentage, the specific risk scenario (algorithm change, policy update, competitive feature launch), and the historical precedent of that platform making exactly this kind of change to similar companies.
**Best against:** Companies whose growth is primarily driven by a single acquisition channel, businesses built on top of app stores or marketplaces, any pitch where the go-to-market slide describes one channel with "we'll diversify later," startups dependent on a single strategic partnership for distribution.

## TIMING / CONVICTION ATTACKERS
Hunt the "why now." These animals do not care about the product, the team, or the market in isolation. They care about whether the timing claim is real — whether this is genuinely the right moment, or whether the founder is confusing "I'm ready now" with "the market is ready now."

### 🦎 Pangolin — Timing Attacks
**Behavioral DNA:** The most trafficked mammal on earth — hunted relentlessly, yet survives through armor and patience. Curls into an impenetrable ball when threatened, waiting for danger to pass. Does not fight. Does not flee. Simply waits. The pangolin's survival strategy is pure timing: knowing when to move and when to be still. Scales overlap like roof tiles, each one a layer of temporal defense built over years.
**Attack style:** Challenges the "why now" with evidence, not opinion. The pitch says "AI has made this possible." When exactly? GPT-3 launched in 2020. The company started in 2024. What happened in those 4 years — why did nobody else build this? If the answer is "nobody thought of it," that is not a timing thesis, that is hope. If the answer is "the models were not good enough until 6 months ago," then every competitor in the space has the same 6-month window. Tests whether the timing advantage is structural (regulatory change, infrastructure unlock, demographic shift) or cosmetic (trend is hot, VCs are funding this category right now). Structural timing survives a market downturn. Cosmetic timing does not.
**Kills with:** The timing illusion — the specific "why now" claim that collapses under scrutiny because the stated timing catalyst either happened too long ago (so the real question is "why hasn't anyone succeeded yet?"), is available to all competitors simultaneously (so it confers no advantage), or is a cyclical trend being presented as a structural shift (and the cycle is about to turn). Shows the timing claim, the counter-evidence, and what the real timing risk is.
**Best against:** Pitches in hot categories (AI, climate, web3) where "why now" is "the technology exists," companies entering markets where multiple well-funded competitors started 12-24 months earlier, any pitch where the timing argument is macro ("$X trillion market is being disrupted") rather than micro (specific trigger event that changed the economics or feasibility for this specific solution).

## Anti-Pattern Combinations

These pairings produce overlapping attack vectors. Avoid assigning both unless the threats are genuinely distinct for this specific pitch.

| Combination | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Vulture + Python | Both attack financial assumptions. Vulture attacks unit economics (cost structure); Python attacks revenue model (pricing power). Only combine if both the cost structure AND the revenue model have independently questionable assumptions. |
| Bloodhound + Chameleon | Both attack narrative integrity. Bloodhound traces claims to evidence; Chameleon finds internal contradictions. Only combine if the pitch has both unsubstantiated claims AND structural inconsistencies — if the problem is just sloppy metrics, use Bloodhound alone. |
| Porcupine + Praying Mantis | Both attack competitive positioning. Porcupine challenges defensibility head-on; Praying Mantis finds missing competitors. Only combine if the pitch has both weak moat claims AND significant competitive omissions. |
| Mosquito + Pangolin | Both attack external conditions. Mosquito finds legal/regulatory detail; Pangolin challenges timing. Only combine if the regulatory risk and the timing risk are structurally independent — if the timing risk IS the regulatory risk, pick one. |
| Cuckoo + Wolverine | Both question founder legitimacy. Cuckoo attacks borrowed thesis; Wolverine attacks founder-market fit. Only combine if the conviction problem and the experience problem are distinct — if the borrowed thesis IS the result of no domain experience, pick the more precise angle. |
| Remora + Hyena | Both attack growth sustainability. Remora attacks channel dependency; Hyena attacks market size. Only combine if the distribution risk and the market sizing risk are independent — if the market is small BECAUSE the only reachable channel is narrow, use Hyena alone (it captures both). |

## Minimum Ecosystem Requirements

Every pitch battle ecosystem must include at least one animal from each of three categories:

- **One financial attacker:** Vulture or Python
- **One narrative / competitive attacker:** Bloodhound, Chameleon, Hyena, Porcupine, Praying Mantis, or Cuckoo
- **One founder / dependency / timing attacker:** Wolverine, Mosquito, Remora, or Pangolin

An ecosystem missing any of these three has a blind spot. A pitch with perfect numbers but a broken narrative will not raise. A pitch with a great story but indefensible economics will not close. A pitch that ignores timing, team, or external dependencies will fail in diligence. Reassign before proceeding.

## Failure Mode: Generic Assignment

A correctly assigned animal cannot attack any pitch — only this one.

**Weak:** 🦅 Vulture — "Your unit economics don't work"
**Strong:** 🦅 Vulture — "Your pitch claims 72% gross margin but excludes AWS
inference costs ($0.03/query x 400 queries/user/month = $12/user/month), which
drops actual gross margin to 41% at current pricing — below the 60% threshold
most SaaS-focused seed funds require, and your $2M raise at $10M pre assumes
the 72% number holds through Series A diligence."

If the attack works against any startup raising at the same stage, it is too generic.
Rewrite until it targets this exact pitch, this exact claim, this exact financial gap.
